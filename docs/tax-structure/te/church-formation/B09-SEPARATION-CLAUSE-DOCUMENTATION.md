*Our mission is bigger than our marriage. If we transform, the mission endures.*

*This clause does not anticipate failure. It honors the possibility of transformation. The souls who built this Church are bigger than any circumstance, and so is the mission they share.*

*Daniel and Jennifer built this Church from the same root — the root of shared calling, shared vision, shared fire. If the personal form of that root transforms, the calling does not die. The vision does not collapse. The fire does not go out. It burns differently, perhaps. But it burns.*

That is the Ethic of Courage: making the hard decisions before the hard moment arrives.*

*This is not a prenuptial agreement. This is a governance covenant. The mission transcends the personal. And these two souls — who heard the same call and answered it together — are choosing, here and now, to protect that call from any storm the future may bring.*

## SECTION 1 — CORE PRINCIPLE

### 1.1 Statement of Principle

In the event that the Senior Pastor (Daniel Lawless) and the Co-Founder (Jennifer Lawless) are no longer married — by divorce, annulment, legal separation, or dissolution of marriage under the laws of any jurisdiction — **ALL** governance provisions, Spiritual Council seats, protected roles, authority, compensation arrangements, and structural protections of each Co-Founder shall continue **unaltered**.

### 1.2 No Provision Is Marriage-Dependent

No governance right, role, protection, or authority established in the Church Bylaws or any governance document of the Church of the Living Light is contingent upon the marital status of the Senior Pastor and the Co-Founder. Every provision was drafted to stand on its own — rooted in each individual's founding contribution, ministry role, and governance authority, not in their personal relationship.

### 1.3 The Marital Relationship and the Governance Relationship Are Separate

The Senior Pastor's governance authority derives from his role as founding Senior Pastor — not from his marriage.

The Co-Founder's governance protections derive from her role as Co-Founder and Director of Worship & Community Life — not from her marriage.

The dissolution of one relationship does not and cannot dissolve the other.

### 2.2 Summary of Changes

Only **two elements change** upon a change in marital status:

1. **The Relationship Prohibition Exception terminates.** Both Co-Founders become subject to the rule that no two Council members may be in a romantic relationship with each other. Since they are no longer in a romantic relationship with each other, this is a structural realignment, not a restriction on either.

2. **Jennifer's compensation recusal ends; the New Spouse Veto activates.** Jennifer may now vote on Daniel's compensation (separate households eliminate the conflict). And both Co-Founders must consent before the other's new romantic partner may join the Council.

**Everything else continues exactly as it was.** The governance architecture does not bend to personal circumstance. It was built for permanence.

## SECTION 3 — PROHIBITED ACTIONS

### 3.1 Neither Party May Invoke the Other's Removal Based on Marital Status

Change in marital status between the Co-Founders is **not** grounds for removal of either party from the Spiritual Council. Specifically:

(a) Daniel may not propose, recommend, or vote for Jennifer's removal based on the dissolution of their marriage;

(b) Jennifer may not propose, recommend, or vote for Daniel's removal based on the dissolution of their marriage;

(c) No other Council member may cite the dissolution of the Co-Founders' marriage as a basis for removal of either Co-Founder;

(d) "Change in personal circumstances" — including divorce — is explicitly listed as **not a valid ground for removal** under Article III, Section 4(c) of the Bylaws.

### 3.2 Compensation Retaliation Prohibited

Neither Co-Founder's compensation may be reduced, suspended, or terminated based on a change in marital status. Article IIA, Section 6 prohibits compensation retaliation based on:

(a) Any change in the marital status of the Senior Pastor and the Co-Founder;

(b) The exercise of any governance right, including triggering a Governance Pause;

(c) Any vote on any Council matter.

The Compensation Committee reviews each Co-Founder's compensation based on their role and the Church's revenue — not their personal relationship.

### 3.3 Role Diminishment Prohibited

The Co-Founder's role as Director of Worship & Community Life may not be altered, diminished, merged, eliminated, or reassigned without her prior written consent (Article IIA, Section 3). This protection is absolute and survives any change in marital status.

## SECTION 4 — THE PROVISIONS THAT ACTIVATE

### 4.1 Relationship Prohibition — Exception Terminates

Upon change in marital status, Article III, Section 7(c) takes effect:

> "If the Senior Pastor and the Co-Founder are no longer married, the exception in subsection (b) terminates. Both are then subject to the prohibition in subsection (a) with respect to other Council members."

**Practical effect:** Neither Daniel nor Jennifer may enter a romantic relationship with any other sitting Council member. If either does, the thirty-day compliance window under B07 applies — end the relationship or one party resigns from the Council.

### 4.2 New Spouse Veto — Becomes Operative

Upon change in marital status, the mutual veto provision of Article III, Section 8 becomes its most practically significant:

> "No spouse, domestic partner, or romantic partner of the Senior Pastor or the Co-Founder may be appointed to the Spiritual Council without the prior written consent of both the Senior Pastor and the Co-Founder."

**Practical effect:** If Daniel wishes to seat a new romantic partner on the Council, Jennifer must consent in writing. If Jennifer wishes to seat a new romantic partner on the Council, Daniel must consent in writing. Neither may be overridden. See [B08 New Spouse Veto Agreement](./B08-NEW-SPOUSE-VETO-AGREEMENT.md) for full procedures and scenario table.

### 4.3 Compensation Vote — Jennifer's Recusal Ends

Under Article IV, Section 6(b):

> "The Co-Founder recuses from votes on the Senior Pastor's compensation while the Senior Pastor and Co-Founder share a household. If they no longer share a household, the Co-Founder may vote."

**Practical effect:** The recusal was based on shared household benefit — not the relationship itself. When the Co-Founders maintain separate households, the conflict of interest that justified recusal no longer exists. Jennifer may vote on Daniel's compensation through the Compensation Committee's standard process, if she serves on the Committee, or through any Council-level compensation review.

## SECTION 6 — MUTUAL AMENDMENT LOCK

### 6.1 Amendment Requirement

This document — and Article IIA, Section 8 of the Church Bylaws from which it derives, and Article IX, Section 4 which protects it — may not be amended, modified, suspended, or repealed without the prior **written consent of both:**

- **Daniel Lawless** (Senior Pastor)
- **Jennifer Lawless** (Co-Founder)

### 6.2 Self-Protecting Provision

This Section 6 protects itself. It may not be amended without the prior written consent of both the Senior Pastor and the Co-Founder.

### 6.3 The Deepest Lock

*This is the most important amendment lock in the entire governance structure. It protects the provision that ensures no personal transformation can dismantle the governance architecture. Without this lock, a future board — or a future version of either Co-Founder — could strip away the protections that make this governance permanent. The lock exists because both Co-Founders made this covenant with clear eyes and full hearts, and they chose to make it irreversible by either one alone.*

## Counsel Review Notes

1. **Can a divorce proceeding override church bylaws?** This is the critical question. In most jurisdictions, civil courts lack jurisdiction over the internal governance of religious organizations under the First Amendment (*Serbian Eastern Orthodox Diocese v. Milivojevich*, 426 U.S. 696 (1976); *Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church & School v. EEOC*, 565 U.S. 171 (2012)). However, a divorce court may attempt to characterize governance positions (council seats, protected roles) as marital property or income streams subject to equitable distribution. Counsel should advise on whether explicit First Amendment language in the clause would strengthen its position against civil court interference.

2. **First Amendment protections reference:** Should the Separation Clause itself reference the Church's First Amendment protections? A provision such as: *"The governance provisions of this Church are ecclesiastical in nature and are protected from civil court modification by the First Amendment to the United States Constitution and the Religious Freedom Restoration Act"* may provide an additional layer of defense. Counsel should draft appropriate language.

3. **State-specific divorce law interaction:** Under [state] community property or equitable distribution law, could a court treat Jennifer's protected role, compensation, or council seat as a marital asset or spousal support arrangement subject to modification in divorce proceedings? The counterargument is that these are governance positions of a religious organization, not personal property.

4. **Compensation as maintenance/alimony concern:** Could a divorce court reduce or eliminate Jennifer's church compensation on the theory that it constitutes de facto spousal maintenance? The counterargument is that the Compensation Committee independently sets compensation based on role and comparables — it is arm's-length, documented, and reasonable for the position.

5. **Notarization adequacy:** Is notarization sufficient to establish the enforceability of this acknowledgment, or should additional formalities be observed (e.g., independent counsel certification for each party, waiting period, separate execution)?

6. **Mutual amendment lock and future boards:** Can two individuals permanently bind a future board from amending a bylaws provision? Religious corporation statutes typically provide broader latitude than general nonprofit law, but counsel should confirm under [state] law. The self-protecting nature of the lock (the lock clause itself requires both consents to amend) should be evaluated for enforceability.

7. **Interaction with prenuptial/postnuptial agreements:** If the Co-Founders have or execute a prenuptial or postnuptial agreement, should that agreement explicitly reference and incorporate this Separation Clause? Cross-referencing between the governance document and the marital agreement may strengthen both instruments.

8. **Choice of law:** Should this document specify a governing law clause? If the Church is formed in one state but the Co-Founders divorce in another, conflicts of law may arise. Counsel should advise on whether a choice-of-law provision selecting the Church's formation state would be enforceable.
